12. Description of project for new applications
Kombinatorische Zufallsstrukturen, Analyse von Algorithmen und Dynamische Systeme

Zusammenfassung:

Dieses Projekt beschäftigt sich mit der Untersuchung des Wechselspiels zwischen kombinatorischen Zufallsstrukturen und dynamischen Systemen. Dies wird angewandt auf die Analyse von Algorithmen mit Hilfe der Theorie dynamischer Systeme. Hierzu betrachten wir zwei verschiedene Zugänge. Wenn man die zeitliche Entwicklung eines Algorithmus als dynamisches System interpretiert, so kann man dessen durchschnittliches Verhalten über die Spektralanalyse eines assoziierten Transferoperators bestimmen. Auf der anderen Seite führt die Analyse einer großen Klasse von balancierten kombinatorischen Strukturen zu asymptotischen Problemen der enumerativen Kombinatorik, die man mit Hilfe von Iterationstheorie behandeln kann.

Combinatorics of Random Structures, Analysis of Algorithms, and Dynamical Systems

Abstract:

This proposal is concerned with the investigation of the interplay between random combinatorial structures and dynamical systems. The main applications envisaged regard the analysis of algorithms using methods from dynamical systems theory.  For this, we investigate two different approaches. Interpreting the time evolution of an algorithm as a dynamical system, one can deduce average-case behavior from the spectral analysis of an associated transfer operator. On the other hand, the analysis of a large class of balanced combinatorial structures leads to asymptotic problems of enumerative combinatorics which can be treated using iteration theory.
 

 French translation: Combinatoire des structures aléatoires, analyse d'algorithmes et systèmes dynamiques

Les objectifs de ce projet se situent à l'interface entre les systèmes dynamiques et les structures combinatoires aléatoires.  Plus précisément, on cherchera à appliquer  des méthodes classiques en systèmes dynamiques à l'analyse d'algorithmes, avec deux différentes approches: D'abord,  quand on  considère un algorithme et  l'ensemble de ses données comme un système dynamique, la description du spectre de l'operateur de transfert associé peut fournir l'analyse du comportement moyen de  l'algorithme. Ensuite, la théorie de l'itération peut permettre de traiter des problèmes asymptotiques en combinatoire énumérative et d'analyser  en particulier une classe importante de structures combinatoires "équilibrées".

The aim of this project is the investigation of the interplay between random combinatorial structures and dynamical systems. We propose to assemble a team of researchers from both Computer Science and Theoretical Physics to benefit from the different expertise which researchers from each discipline can bring to bear upon this project. The main applications envisaged regard the analysis of algorithms, principally, algorithms in computational number theory and in information theory.

The Clausthal group of  theoretical physicists/mathematicians has extensive competencies in iteration theory and dynamical systems, including the theory of dynamical zeta functions, spectral analysis of transfer operators, and applications thereof to continued fraction transformations [Mayer1980,Mayer1990,Prellberg1991]. The French group of computer scientists has developed a strong specialty in the analysis of the major algorithms and data structures in computer science ([Sedgewick1996]).; the problems there reduce largely to the analysis of probabilistic properties of random discrete structures. It has been realized recently  that there are strong connections between the two areas: the time evolution of an algorithm can be interpreted as a dynamical system, whence methods from dynamical systems theory are applicable. Using the thermodynamic formalism, the algorithms of interest can then even be analyzed via statistical mechanical methods ([DaFlaVa97], [FlaVa98], [FlaVa00], [Va97], [Va98], [Va00], [Va01] ).

Members of the two groups have been already in contact for a few years. Brigitte Vallée from the Université de Caen has been invited to Clausthal in 1998 for a scientific visit with Dieter Mayer. Thomas Prellberg has visited Philippe Flajolet at INRIA in 1995 and has had numerous exchanges with Flajolet on the occasion of conferences. On the occasion of a joint invitation of Flajolet and Vallée to Clausthal in 2001, the present project has been conceived. In the course of this project, doctoral students and post-doctoral researchers will be involved.

Beyond the technical problems discussed below, the proposal aims at the elucidation of very basic questions such as: Why is the Euclidean algorithm ''Gaussian''? Which of the Euclidean algorithms share this feature? How well do proposed optimizations of digital tries (a universal data structure for textual data and dictionaries) fare?

It is quite natural to view an algorithm together with its data as the iteration of a global transformation. Then, it is reasonable to expect that methods developed to analyze the iteration of a transformation should be applicable to the analysis of algorithms. Indeed, Vallée has made use of the transfer operator in order to analyze a broad class of algorithms. This covers algorithms of the broad Euclidean type (where the Ruelle-Mayer operators are essential) as well as some of the most efficient tree data structures of information theory. The results obtained via this path are necessarily probabilistic in nature, and one gets information on the average-case behaviour of the algorithm in question. On another, quite complementary, aspect, iteration theory has proved very useful in the analysis of a large class of balanced combinatorial structures (Prellberg-Flajolet). Here, one considers asymptotic problems of enumerative combinatorics.

The research program consists of  two items:

  1. Transfer operators and analysis of algorithms

    2. Until now, transfer operators have been used  for evaluating expectations of parameters, which turn out to be related to dominant eigenvalue properties of operators. Examples include the number of steps of the Euclidean algorithm or path length in digital tries. When extending average case analyses to distributional ones, a precise knowledge of the entire spectrum of the transfer operator is needed. For general data, one can also envisage a multifractal analysis of the algorithm behavior in terms of the scaling behavior of the underlying data distribution. We propose to investigate computational aspects related to the spectrum, a difficult conjecture on the geometric decay of eigenvalues, and also the parameter values for which the operator has eigenvalue one. Techniques to do so may borrow from perturbation theory of operators, functional analysis, and numerical mathematics. Another question is whether the centered continued fraction algorithms has a hidden Hilbert space structure and consequently a real spectrum. Other problems to be considered are higher dimensional generalizations, e.g., the lattice reduction algorithms or the simultaneous approximation problem that are ubiquitous in computational number theory. One essential difficulty which needs to be overcome here is that the algorithm acts on a higher-dimensional data set. Even though this has been accomplished for simple two-dimensional examples, the general case is much less understood.
     
  2. Iteration theory and random discrete structures

    3. Methods of analytic combinatorics have been developed by the Paris group. Such methods are well suited to the investigation of probabilistic properties of combinatorial objects that are sufficiently ''context-free''. On the other hand, balanced structures, despite its importance in applications (e.g., 2-3 trees and B-trees) have been a stumbling block of the theory. Preliminary investigations by Flajolet and Prellberg indicate the possibility to analyze such structures via classical iteration theory. An example of the analysis of a recursive algorithm has been discussed in [Prellberg2001]. In this case, the iteration of a fixed function models the growth process of a tree at its leaves. It seems that quite general methods could be developed in this area leading to a classification of balanced tree structures based on simple analytic characteristics of a base function. Various enumerative results should follow from this approach that should also open the way to average case analysis of major parameters (like size, height, etc.), and even to distributional estimates. In the area of random discrete structures, another important classification problem is: To what extent does ''coalescence'' represent of a universality class amongst discrete structures and algorithms? The idea there is to develop a unified treatment of hashing tables that fill, path length in various types of trees, emergence of the giant component in the random graph, and many more.
